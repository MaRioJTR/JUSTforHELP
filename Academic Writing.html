    Supporting Sentences

    Major Idea (MA)

    starters are phrases or techniques used to introduce the primary points or arguments within a piece of writing. They typically outline the main themes or topics that the writer intends to address, providing a roadmap for the reader to follow. Major idea starters help establish the structure and focus of the writing.

    Examples

    First, Second, Third.... etc

    The main argument is...

    One key point to consider is...

    An important aspect of this topic is...

    A significant finding in this research is...

    It is essential to understand that...

    Minor Idea (MI)

    starters are phrases or techniques used to introduce supporting details, evidence, or examples that expand upon the major ideas presented in a piece of writing. They provide additional information or clarification to strengthen the writer's arguments or assertions. Minor idea starters contribute to the depth and coherence of the writing by offering specific examples or explanations.

    Examples

    Furthermore, ...

    Moreover, ...

    In addition, ...

    Additionally, ...

    Another point to consider is...

    Additionally, it is worth noting that...

    For instance, ...

    Moreover, an example of this can be seen in...

    Transition Signals

    are words or phrases used to link ideas, sentences, or paragraphs together within a piece of writing. They help readers understand the flow of information and the relationship between different parts of the text. Transition signals can indicate a variety of relationships, including cause and effect, comparison and contrast, addition, concession, time sequence, and more.

    Examples

    Addition: Additionally, furthermore, moreover, also, in addition, besides.

    Cause and Effect: Consequently, thus, therefore, as a result, hence, because, since.

    Comparison and Contrast: Similarly, likewise, on the other hand, however, in contrast, whereas, nevertheless.

    Time Sequence: First, second, third, finally, next, then, afterward, meanwhile.

    Concession: However, nevertheless, despite, although, even though, though.

    Illustration/Example: For example, for instance, to illustrate, in particular.

    Summary/Conclusion: In conclusion, to summarize, overall, all in all, in summary.

    Types of Sentences

    Simple

    Compound

    Complex

    Simple :

    Simply, has only one sbject and one verb

    Note if there was "sub And sub" it consider as one subject

    Independent clause and meaningful idea

    Compound :

    It has 2 independent clausese connected by coordinator

    Example

    I like coffe, and she likes tea

    I like coffe ; she likes tea

    I like coffe but she likes tea

    coordinators

    For: Indicates reason or cause.

    And: Joins elements that are similar or add information.

    Nor: Connects two negative alternatives.

    But: Introduces a contrast or exception.

    Or: Indicates a choice or alternative.

    Yet: Indicates contrast or contradiction.

    So: Shows result, consequence, or purpose.

    Complex :

    one independent + one dependent

    Starts with subordinator

    Example

    If (subordinator) i get up early (dep) , i will catch the bus (indep) .

    Subordinators

    After: After I finish my homework, I'll go for a walk.

    Although: Although it was raining, we decided to go for a hike.

    Because: I ate an apple because I was hungry.

    If: If you study hard, you'll pass the exam.

    Since: Since I don't have any plans, I'll stay home tonight.

    Though: Though it was late, he continued working.

    While: I listened to music while I cleaned the house.
